Life Saving Protocol - Machine Guarding

Purpose

  1. Space has developed a set of mandatory Life Saving Protocols that set a minimum standard and minimum performance expectations for managing high-level hazards common to the glass industry.
  2. Machine safeguarding is essential when work requires that some part of the workerâ€™s body is placed in the path of machine movement, or within the vicinity of moving parts. Machine safeguarding prevents injury from the following sources:
    1. Direct contact with the moving parts of a machine.
    2. Contact with hot metals, chemicals, or ejection of foreign objects generated from machine-tool operations.
    3. Mechanical and electrical failures, such as power outages.
    4. Human failure that can result from curiosity, distraction, fatigue, worry, anger, illness and deliberate risk-taking.
  3. In general, the most important objective of machine safeguarding is to protect the operator at the point of operation, to protect colleagues when they are close to point of operation and to protect maintenance personnel when performing intervention work.

Scope

  1. This protocol applies to all Space employees, contractors and visitors, and all operations including manufacturing facilities, technical centers, offices & administrative buildings. All sites in all regions are expected to develop site specific procedures to comply with this protocol.
  2. This protocol identifies the minimum requirements associated with guarding for machines, plant and equipment.
  3. Requirements apply to machines, plant and equipment used in the workplace to perform functions which include but not limited to cutting, drilling, punching, grinding, pressing, forming, hammering, joining, molding, combining, mixing, sorting, packaging and assembling. The protocol also applies to machines used in for routine and non-routine activities.

Process Requirements

  1. Formal machines, plant and equipment guarding processes and procedures must be established to prevent contact with plant and equipment hazard sources, which includes exposure to; mechanical motion, electricity, heat and chemicals.
  2. Formally identify hazards and risks associated with the erection, installation, commissioning, operation, inspection, maintenance, repair, service and cleaning of any new and existing machine for use in the workplace.
  3. Formally assess the risks (likelihood versus consequence) associated with the identified hazards. Typical hazards that should be assessed include; trapping, entanglement, shearing, cutting, impact, crushing, stabbing, puncturing, friction, abrasion, temperature, electrocution and ejection.
  4. Consider the hierarchy of control when developing controls.
  5. Ensure that the appropriate type of guarding is used. The range of guards includes, but is not limited to, permanently fixed physical barriers, interlocked and presence-sensing systems.
  6. Guards, paneling, or safety interlocks must not be tampered with or removed without first isolating energy sources to equipment.
  7. The guard shall be such that it does not facilitate or cause an accident in itself.
  8. Existing equipment must be systematically assessed based on: current level of risk, hazard, near miss and incident data and local regulatory requirements.
  9. Upon installation, guards and other safety controls must be regularly inspected and maintained. At a minimum annual inspections must be undertaken and documented, and the documentation must be retained.
  10. If the design of the guarding includes an emergency stop, the stop control must be clearly marked in local language, durable, accessible, colour coded and cannot be adversely affected by electrical malfunction.
  11. Emergency stops shall not to be used as a substitute to isolation.
  12. The unauthorised removal of guarding from plant or equipment or failure to restore guarding upon completion of work is a direct contravention of EHS Golden Rule 6.
  13. Where it is not practicable to provide guarding and people are required to operate or pass close to dangerous moving parts, a formal risk assessment and safe system of work must be documented and effectively implemented to reduce risks.
  14. Competency based training and instruction must be provided as necessary for employees, managers, supervisors, machine operators, maintenance personnel and anyone else interacting with plant and equipment in a manner that is readily understood.
  15. Competency based training should include; machine hazards, machines guarding and how they provide protection, how to use the guards and when guards can be removed and by whom.
