Batch House Building - Pest Control

Purpose

To define a procedure to establish batch house building pest control guidelines for an Space plant.

Procedure

  1. Pest Control – General
    1. Prevention:
      1. Adequate pest control measures must be taken to:
        1. Prevent the entrance of pests.
        2. Deny pests harborage, food or water.
        3. Detect and eliminate pests as necessary.
      2. If bordering properties not under Space control present undue risk to product integrity, additional monitoring, extermination and building maintenance shall be initiated so as to exclude and control pests, dirt and other potential contaminants originating from such non-controllable areas.
    2. Activity Logs
      1. A map of the facility property shall be maintained that indicates:
        1. Locations of all traps and monitoring devices.
        2. Pest activity for the current and previous year including date of occurrence.
          1. If desired, separate maps may be maintained for each year’s activity.Maps may be paper, whiteboard, or electronic. If electronic tracking is used, tracking charts are an acceptable alternative to the physical map, as the purpose is to identify trends.
          2. Maps may be paper, whiteboard, or electronic. If electronic tracking is used, tracking charts are an acceptable alternative to the physical map, as the purpose is to identify trends.
    3. Monitoring Frequency
      1. Pest control devices shall be monitored at a frequency sufficient to prevent infestation. This frequency shall be determined by the cooperative efforts of the plant management and the pest control operator.
        1. The minimum monitoring frequency shall not be less than once per month.
  2. Rodent Control
    1. Use and Control of Rodent Monitoring Devices
      1. Bait stations, mechanical traps and glueboards shall be monitored regularly. Accurate records must be kept of their condition and effectiveness.
        1. The minimum monitoring frequency shall not be less than once per month.
      2. Each bait station and mechanical trap shall:
        1. Be code-marked for location reference.
        2. Have a service date tag or UPC code affixed to the inside of the top cover.
        3. Have corresponding wall markers to readily identify the locations of missing devices.
      3. Bait stations shall be of an EPA-approved tamper resistant design, and be durable and moisture-resistant.
      4. Bait stations shall be locked and anchored to the ground or building to prevent disruption by non-target animals.
      5. The use of toxic bait inside of buildings should only be considered as a last resort, and implemented only after increased non-baited trapping has proven ineffective.
      6. Non-toxic baits (e.g., peanut butter on a snap trap) may be used if baited traps are deemed necessary.
      7. EHS and the plant's Good Manufacturing Practices (GMP) coordinator must be contacted prior to the use of any toxic bait inside.
    2. Exterior Perimeter of Buildings
      1. Perimeter Attributes
        1. The surface perimeters surrounding buildings used for the production of or storage of glass shall be constructed of a hard surface (concrete, asphalt, small stones, small cullet, or similar material). This discourages rodent burrowing, promotes good drainage, impedes vegetation growth, and aids in clean up.
        2. The perimeters shall extend at least 3 feet (0.9 meters) beyond the bases of the building walls.
        3. Plastic or nylon mesh ground cover shall not be used under a stone or cullet perimeter.
      2. Bait Stations
        1. Bait stations shall be placed at an initial spacing of 50 to 75 feet (15.2 to 22.8 meters). This spacing shall be periodically reviewed and adjusted as necessary in response to pest activity data.
      3. Building Integrity
        1. Windows in warehouses, production departments, and support departments adjacent to Production lines shall be fitted with screens.
          1. The Forming department and Batch and Furnace department are exempt from the screening requirement.
      4. Exterior Housekeeping
        1. There shall be no accumulation of drums, pallets, machinery, lumber, boxes, trash, high grass or weeds. All of these may provide protected areas for pests.
        2. Pallets, boards or other similar materials shall not be placed against outside walls. Doing so provides easy access for rodents or other vermin, and impedes inspection and placement of traps.
  3. Bird Control
    1. Bird control can be difficult. Like rodent control, bird control should incorporate an integrated pest management approach. Many quick fix ideas have been developed, with very few showing any degree of long-term success. Some of these control measures include the use of noisemakers, flashing lights, imitation owls or snakes, sticky roosts, etc. The most successful bird control programs include the following:
      1. Regular removal and destruction of all nests of resident birds. The removal program should be ongoing to prevent the building of new nests. Persistence is required, and several nest removal/rebuilding cycles may be necessary before the birds will become discouraged and vacate the area.
      2. Bird droppings must be promptly cleaned up upon discovery, and increased surveillance must take place to prevent recurrence.
        1. Bird droppings can present significant health problems. Consult the local pest control operator or a professional industrial cleaning service if extensive cleanup is necessary.
