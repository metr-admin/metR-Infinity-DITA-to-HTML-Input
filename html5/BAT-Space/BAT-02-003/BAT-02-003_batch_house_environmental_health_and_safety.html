Batch House Environmental Health and Safety

Purpose

To define a standard set of mandatory minimum environmental, health and safety requirements and procedures that apply to all personnel working in the Batch House at all plants.

Procedure

Plant leadership shall be accountable and responsible for providing the environmental, health and safety devices and equipment, prerequisite programs, and procedures and environmental, health and safety assessments described and referred to in this document.

Environmental
  1. All plants shall develop and manage procedures in compliance with the EHS Air Emissions Management Fundamental as required by specific job duties. The procedure shall include the following Batch House specific requirements:
    General
    1. Efficient dust management equipment including transferring equipment, gathering equipment and raw materials silos in the Batch House to control dust.
    2. Good work practices to minimize fugitive emissions during unloading and material transfer.
    3. Good work practices to clean-up batch unloading area when material is spilled.
    4. Good work practices to manage waste batch material (the handling of waste batch material can be problematic - fugitive emissions and spills).
    5. Periodic inspections of the unloading process to confirm that fugitive dust during unloading is not going across the property line.
    6. Comply with any permit conditions regarding fugitive emissions in the raw material unloading and handling process and within the Batch House.

      Within the Batch House

    7. Conduct a periodic inspection of each filter sock and dust collector that is used in the Batch House.
    8. Promptly perform corrective actions to remedy the situation (no excess discharge outside) when the dust collection equipment is not working properly.
    9. Document inspections, problems found and the corrective actions taken.
    10. If required by the permit, complete Visual Determination of Visible Emissions Readings at the Batch House.

      Modifications

    11. Do not add equipment or replace existing equipment in the Batch House without determining the need for a permit or permit modification.
  2. All plants shall develop and manage procedures in compliance with the EHS Waste Management Fundamental as required by specific job duties. The procedure shall include the following Batch House specific requirements:
    1. Properly characterize all hazardous and non-hazardous materials and ensure they are kept separate. Waste batch and cullet will require additional management if they are a hazardous waste. Do not mix hazardous and non-hazardous waste.
    2. Housekeeping of outside batch area is critical to overall waste management compliance. This area should be regularly swept with equipment capable of vacuuming, wetting and collecting material during sweeping operation. Dry sweeping is prohibited. The waste should be separated as needed. Where sweeping is not possible, spilled raw material and batch should be shoveled up or otherwise removed promptly.
    3. Provide appropriate storage areas to handle waste batch, waste cullet and basement cullet. Do not typically mix wastes together or add construction and miscellaneous debris to designated waste areas.
  3. All plants shall develop and manage procedures in compliance with the EHS Storm Water Pollution Prevention Plan Fundamental (SWPPP) as required by specific job duties. The procedure shall include the following Batch House specific requirements:
    1. The batch/raw material transfer conveyer and elevator should be contained/covered to minimize leakage, routinely inspected for proper operations including routinely checking for proper tracking and leakage should be promptly cleaned up.
    2. The raw material unloading area should be swept after each raw material delivery and vacuumed as needed. Measures should be taken to minimize wind from blowing material around.
    3. Need to control water run off (dry weather and storm water) from basement cullet outside bunkers. Operations should consolidate storage area, protect area from storm water, and direct runoff back to basement for reuse.
    4. Ensure that a dust collector system is utilized, as needed, to control fugitive dust from various operations to protect these materials from storm water impact, waste management concerns and air permit concerns.
  4. All plants shall develop and manage oil management procedures in compliance with the EHS Spill Prevention Control and Countermeasure Plan (SPCC) and implement best management practices as required by specific job duties.
  5. All plants shall develop and manage asbestos procedures in compliance with the EHS Asbestos Management Plan guidance as required by specific job duties. The procedure shall include the following Batch House specific requirements:
    1. Follow “Do Not Disturb” instructions/signs near any asbestos containing materials commonly found in window caulking and paint at the Batch House.

    Health

    Respirable Crystalline Silica (RCS) is one of the most impactful health concerns relative to Space Batch Houses. RCS primarily comes from the sand portion of the batch mix, which can become airborne anytime the batch material is not contained and/or disturbed during the process, cleaning, or maintenance activities. Airborne concentrations must be evaluated and controls put in place according to the Global EHS Respirable Silica Fundamental EHS-06-004.

  6. Each facility shall conduct a respirable crystalline silica (RCS) exposure assessment to determine the potential for employee exposure to RCS. The assessment shall be based on the potential RCS airborne concentrations for the jobs and tasks of similar exposure groups (SEGs).
  7. Each facility shall prepare a sampling plan based upon the exposure assessment and the plan should cover planned sampling activities over the next 24 to 36 months.
  8. The assessment and sampling plan must be completed by a person competent in the practice of industrial hygiene.
  9. Each facility shall institute a RCS medical surveillance program for all employees who are routinely exposed (i.e., 30 days per year) over the occupational (OEL) based on the results from the exposure assessment and the sampling plan.
  10. Each facility shall have an exposure control plan, or its equivalent. An example is shown in Appendix B6 of EHS-06-004. The written plan shall contain the following elements:
    1. A description of the workplace tasks that involve exposure to respirable crystalline silica.
    2. A description of the engineering controls, work practices, and respiratory protection used to limit employee exposure to respirable crystalline silica for each task.
    3. A description of the housekeeping measures used to limit employee exposure to respirable crystalline silica, such as not allowing dry sweeping or the use of compressed air, and the use of dust suppressant and vacuum systems with HEPA filter collection.
  11. Regulated areas shall be established wherever exposure to airborne concentrations of RCS is (or can reasonably be expected to be) in excess of the local/regional legal exposure limit, as adjusted for extended shifts. The regulated area must be marked, have limited access, and proper PPE must be worn to enter, which will include proper respiratory protection.
  12. Airborne RCS and accumulations of dust shall be reduced by following good housekeeping practices and reporting leaks/spills to a supervisor. When completing Batch House housekeeping, the following methods shall be employed in the following preferred order:
    1. High-efficiency particulate air filter (HEPA) vacuuming shall be the preferred method and shall be used where practical.
      1. When central vacuum equipment is used, it should be evaluated to confirm proper function following the form shown in Appendix B9 (or its equivalent) shall be completed to evaluate the Batch House equipment performance.
    2. If HEPA vacuuming is not practical, a wet method shall be used.
      1. Floor sweep absorbents and other methods shall be applied. DRY SWEEPING IS PROHIBITED!
    3. If the shoveling of material is necessary, respiratory protection and Tyvek (or a regional equivalent) coverall is required to keep dust off of clothing. To reduce potential dusting when shoveling, material shall be placed in a container or in a chute instead of forceful throwing.
    4. Compressed air shall not be used wherever it could disturb dust (i.e., not to clean or to blow off clothing).
  13. The hierarchy of controls must be followed relative to RCS exposure. Engineering controls must be considered before the use of PPE. The RCS fundamental contains an internal Engineering Design Standard for new batch projects of 25 µg/m3 8-hour TWA.

    Noise exposure in a Batch House is often greater than the applicable occupational exposure limits. Batch House employee are typically included in the site hearing conservation program. The requirements of the Hearing Conservation Program are contained in EHS-02-133.

  14. Each facility shall be complete an assessment and develop a sampling plan to evaluate employees potential exposure to noise in the Batch House and to identify employees for inclusion in the Hearing Conservation Program (HCP). All persons working in areas or with equipment that have noise levels equal to or exceeding 85 decibels (dB) as an 8-hour time-weighted average (TWA), or any locally more stringent legal limit, must be included in the HCP.
  15. Employees included in the HCP must receive medical surveillance and audiograms according to the Country Group or locally more stringent requirements. This typically includes initial tests and periodic retests such as annually.
  16. The hierarchy of controls must be followed when levels exceed 85 dBA or a locally more stringent limit. Engineering controls, followed by administrative controls must be considered before choosing PPE as the last choice.
  17. When choosing hearing protection devices (HPDs), the site must document that hearing protection is adequate based on the assessed noise levels and the local legal requirements.
  18. Signs must be posted indicating the need for PPE where HPDs are required. Signs must meet local legal requirements.
  19. Employees must receive documented initial and annual noise training. Training shall include the risks of elevated noise exposure and proper use of hearing protection, at a minimum.

    Respiratory protection is required in the Batch House when engineering and administrative controls fail to control RCS (and sometimes other contaminants) below occupational exposure limits and sometimes during the completion of specified tasks. The requirements of the Global Respiratory Protection Fundamental is contained in EHS-06-009.

  20. Each site must complete an assessment to determine where respiratory protection is required and select the proper respirators based upon that assessment. The assessment will take into account exposure monitoring that is completed as part of the sites IH assessment and sampling plan.
  21. Before wearing a respirator, employees must receive a medical approval or fit for work certificate signifying the employee can wear a respirator in accordance to EHS-06-009.
  22. Negative pressure respirators (i.e., filtering face piece, half and full face cartridge) respirators require the user to have a documented fit test before initial use and periodically after in accordance to EHS-06-009.
  23. Each site must have a policy in place requiring that nothing (including facial hair) interfere with the seal of a negative pressure respirator. A positive pressure respirator (i.e., PAPR) that does not rely on a facial seal can be worn where compliance cannot be met with the use of a negative pressure respirator.
  24. Respirators must be properly maintained, stored, discarded, and have a documented cartridge change schedule. The cartridge change schedule is based upon the contaminate levels in the work area and indicates when the cartridge should be changed.
  25. The requirement to wear a respirator must be posted on signs in the work area and/or work area entrance.
  26. Employees must receive annual respiratory protection training that meets local regulatory requirements and the requirements contained in EHS-06-009.

    Temperature extremes in a the Batch House varies based on the sites location and seasonal changes. Each site must assess the potential temperature extremes and implement actions as necessary in compliance with Heat Related Illness EHS-02-134.

  27. Each site must complete an assessment to identify employees and tasks that put employees at risk for heat stress. The assessment must meet the requirements contained in EHS-02-134 or the local legal requirements if more stringent.
  28. A written site specific program should be available detailing how the site manages areas or tasks identified at risk during the assessment.
  29. When areas or tasks are identified at risk, then additional provisions for water consumption and cooling areas must be provided per EHS-02-134.
  30. Medical surveillance must be implemented where there is a local legal requirement.
  31. Documented training must be provided annually for all supervisors of employees and employees who have been identified at risk during the assessment. The training must cover, as a minimum, all of the topics contained in EHS-02-134 and as locally required.

    The Batch House has a number of raw materials and other chemicals available for use with potentials for personal employee exposure and exposure to the environment. Each site must ensure the safe and proper use of hazardous and nonhazardous chemicals and must comply with locally applicable regulations addressing the storage and disposal of these chemicals, and the requirements contained in Chemical Management EHS-02-123.

  32. Each site must have a system in place to identify and review chemical uses for all existing and new chemicals. The assessment must take into account the safety of the chemical, potential exposure to employees, exposure to the environment, proper storage, and proper disposal. The review and assessment process must meet the requirements contained within EHS-02-123 and any local legal requirement.
  33. The site must have a current list of all chemical substance and a current SDS for each chemical. The list and SDSs must be available to employees.
  34. Chemical containers must have proper labeling which complies with GHS (Global Harmonization System) requirements and/or according to local legal requirements.
  35. Employees must receive annual training relative to the chemicals they work with, the assessments completed, PPE required, storage, disposal, and handling requirements in addition to any local legal training requirements.

    The Batch House typically has a number of areas that are frequently and sometime infrequently visited for various tasks and at different lighting levels. Site lighting levels should be in compliance with Illumination OIES-L-001 or local requirements if more stringent.

  36. Each site should review the Global Engineering lighting requirements in Illumination OIES-L-001. A plan should be in place to meet the minimum illumination requirements. A system must be in place to promptly replace faulty lighting fixtures and burnt out bulbs.
  37. Illumination levels shall be reviewed and taken into account during all new installations.

    Safety

  38. All Batch House personnel shall wear personal protection equipment (PPE), including, safety shoes, safety glasses and site specific equipment including, but not limited to head and respiratory protection.
  39. All plants shall develop machine-specific energy isolation procedures for all applicable energy sources for the existing Batch House equipment and Lockout-Tagout procedures must be followed if maintenance or cleaning is to be performed. When it is not possible to isolate all energy sources, the plant shall use mechanical blocking devices and administrative procedure to ensure all body parts are protected from moving equipment and unexpected startup of equipment. The plants must meet or exceed the requirements in EHS-02-107-F006.
  40. All machines that currently have guards must have them installed and operational at all times while the equipment is running in normal production operations. Guards that are removed for maintenance or when troubleshooting the equipment must be reinstalled after completion of the maintenance task and before the equipment is returned to normal operations. For any equipment that does not have designed guards, administrative procedures must be visible and clearly understood. The plants must meet or exceed the requirements in EHS-02-122-F002.
  41. When working at heights or near wall or floor openings.
    1. Risk Assessments must be completed when employees are working in a new area or performing non routine tasks.
    2. Fall protection devices must be used where the potential exists for an employee to fall at an unprotected height of 4 feet (1.2 meters) or higher.
    3. Ensure all proper fall protection equipment inspections and training are completed and documented.
    4. Ensure employees are using approved personal lifting devices tied off at all times.
    5. Completion of permits as identified by local plant programs.
    6. When working at heights, the plants must meet or exceed the requirements in EHS-02-103-F004.
    7. If roof access is required, the plants must meet or exceed the requirements in EHS-06-007.
  42. All Hot Work Operations including; Welding, Grinding, Cutting and Torching must meet or exceed the requirements in EHS-108-F002 and include the completion of the Hot Work Permit.
  43. All plants shall develop and manage a plan to ensure industrial equipment and site required vehicle traffic routes are clearly marked and understood.
    1. Ensure the layout of the workspace effectively separate pedestrians, vehicles and powered mobile equipment.
    2. Keep pedestrians and moving vehicles apart (for example, physical barriers, exclusion zones and safety zones).
    3. High visibility personal protective vest or clothing to be worn in areas where powered mobile equipment operate.
    4. Develop formal site specific training programs for traffic management.
    5. The plants must meet or exceed requirements in 02-115-F002.
  44. Plant personnel shall have documented training records and a training plan for Batch House safety, which shall be reviewed annually by Human Resources (HR) and the department supervisor/manager.
  45. Safety programs and procedures shall be implemented and practiced for all Batch House personnel that include, but are not limited to, the following:
    1. No loose clothing, no jewelry, and no hair extending beyond the shoulder. Long hair should be tucked under the helmet/cap, or covered in a bandana.
    2. A qualified first aid responder and a first aid kit are located on site and available at all times. Injuries, incidents, and near misses shall be reported in a timely manner.
    3. Batch House housekeeping program is implemented following the guidelines in Procedure BAT-02-004.
  46. Plant procedures shall address how contractors and visitors will be informed of, and comply with, the plant’s environmental, health and safety requirements.
