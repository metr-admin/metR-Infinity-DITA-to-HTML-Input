PFC UPS Topology

Profiles for UL and IEC are in this topic

Four different stages form one power module:

  • PFC stage with dual functionality combined as PFC rectifier and step-up converter

  • Battery charger stage (step-down converter) with very low voltage ripple

  • Inverter stage

Characteristics:

  • Power factor correction (PFC) rectifier with THDi < 5% and PF >0.96

  • High efficiency with transformer less topology

  • Full digital signal processing (DSP)

PFC Rectifier

Characteristics:

  • The PFC rectifier reduces the level of input harmonics (<5% THDi)

  • No distortion to upstream equipment

PFC Rectifier in AC Operation

Rectifier principle:

  • MOSFET based

  • A calculated reference current with a small ripple band control 20%

  • Fixed frequency, 40 kHz

  • The amplitude of the input current reference is a function of the DC-bus voltage

  • Each half wave has its own converter (diode rectification)

Depending on the required DC-bus voltage, a voltage controller calculates the required/related input current (the necessary input current amplitude to achieve the required DC-bus voltage). A current controller then generates a sinusoidal input current that is synchronous to the input voltage. This is realized by a ripple band controller with a constant switching frequency (40 kHz).

Control loops control the amplitude (supply of the required energy), and creates a current curve that follows the voltage sine wave (creates a linear, resistive load).

PFC Rectifier in DC Operation

Two boost converters (DC/DC) are installed in series (battery midpoint not connected). The battery midpoint stabilization is ensured by a control algorithm.

As the inverter stage only works with a DC input voltage of ±400 (800 VDC), a boost converter (step-up) is required to increase the 400 V battery voltage to the required 800 VDC.

As the inverter stage only works with a DC input voltage of ±225 (450 VDC), a boost converter (step-up) is required to increase the 384 V battery voltage to the required 450 VDC.

Step-Up Converter (Boost Converter)

Step-up converter principle:

  • MOSFET based

  • Output voltage, UA, with a ripple band control

  • Fixed frequency: 40 kHz

  • The amplitude of the input current reference is a function of the DC-bus voltage

Depending on the required DC-bus voltage, a voltage controller calculates the related input current reference (the necessary input current amplitude to achieve the required DC-bus voltage). A current controller then generates a constant input current. As in AC operation, this is realized by a ripple band controller with a constant switching frequency (40 kHz). As the system uses a battery without midpoint, the DC midpoint/neutral) must be artificially created and stabilized, also during battery operation.
In a boost converter, the output voltage UA is always higher than the input voltage UE. The key principle is the behaviour of an inductor L in series with a diode D (free-wheeling) and a capacitor C in connection with a fast acting switch.

  • Switch closed: Current flows through the inductor, and a magnetic field will be built up

  • Switch open: The inductor tries to keep the current flowing in the same direction causing the voltage on the right side of the inductor to increase fast and become higher than the UA across the capacitor. At this stage the diode is closed, and while the magnetic field is slowly reduced, the current keeps flowing and thereby charging the capacitor.

  • Switch closed (capacitor is charged): The capacitor provides voltage and power to the load. At this stage, the blocking diode prevents discharge of the capacitor through the switch. The switch must be opened fast enough again to prevent the capacitor from discharging too deep.

If the switch is closed fast enough, the magnetic field of the inductor will not be fully retrenched between the cycling stages, and the voltage across the capacitor and thus at the load will remain higher than the input voltage.

Battery Charger Step-Down Converter (Buck Converter)

To charge the batteries with the correct voltage (nominal battery voltage 400 V), a voltage step-down converter (buck converter) must be used. The functional principle is similar to that of the boost converter.

To charge the batteries with the correct voltage (nominal battery voltage 384 V), a voltage step-down converter (buck converter) must be used. The functional principle is similar to that of the boost converter.

Battery charger step-down converter principle:

  • MOSFET based

  • No connected battery midpoint

  • Switching frequency, 36.6 kHz

  • Low ripple

  • Battery charge power limiter (max. 20% of system power rating)

  • Midpoint stabilization by control algorithm

  • Line drop and temperature compensation by control algorithm

Additionally, the advanced battery monitor (ABM) is an option and offers the following features:

  • Detection of weak or defective batteries

  • Estimation of capacity and runtime based on voltage and current (runtime is adjusted if a battery that is not fully charged is connected)

3-Level Inverter

3-level inverter principle:

  • MOSFET and IGBT based

  • Ripple band controller

  • Fixed frequency: 20 kHz

  • Integrated inverter output filter

  • High efficiency

  • Short-circuit protection

  • Power limitation based on kVA and kW (the system is phi 0.8 optimized)

In a 3-level (3 poles) inverter system, a stabilized, symmetrical neutral line is required. Generally, there are two voltage levels; a positive DC voltage (+400 VDC) against neutral, and a negative DC (-400 VDC) against neutral. In a 3-level inverter system, higher switching frequency can be released which together with other factors leads to higher efficiency.
